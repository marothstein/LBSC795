Phase 1: Victim Finder Proposal

Team Members

Matthew Rothstein

Project Problem

The project is inspired by both the recent disaster in Japan and by some of my work dealing with disaster preparedness at the NIH over the last three years.

The tsunami that devastated Northern Japan did more than level terrain and buildings, it physically took people away. In addition, the disaster overwhelmed the cellular telephone networks, leaving Japan’s residents unable to use their most common means of individual communication. Without this technology, people turned to social mediums such as Facebook and Twitter to communicate with their family and friends#. As a result, these services proved invaluable, helping some people find their loved ones as they also had access to the medium. On the other hand, many victims of the tsunami were either lost or injured and did not have access to social mediums like those mentioned above. In this case, people will be looking on Facebook or Twitter for individuals unable to look back.

VictimFinder will allow people to still leverage the Facebook platform in finding their loved ones who are lost or injured casualties of a disaster. By utilizing the Face.com face identification API, and databases of victim photos such as that being put together by the Lost Person Finder project at the NIH, I hope to be able to ‘locate’ lost individuals through automated facial comparison between victim photos and tagged photos on Facebook.

Target Users

There are two major user groups involved in this project:

  1. Family and friends of victims lost or injured in a disaster or emergency situation
  2. Disaster response organizations taking photos of victims found or being treated
